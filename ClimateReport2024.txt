Global Climate Report 2024 – Executive Summary
================================================
Published by: International Environmental Research Institute
Date: March 2024

Overview
--------
This report synthesises the latest climate science findings for policymakers, researchers, and the public. Global average surface temperatures in 2023 reached 1.45°C above pre-industrial levels, making it the warmest year on record since measurements began in 1850. The scientific consensus is clear: human-induced greenhouse gas emissions are the primary driver of observed warming.

Key Findings
------------

1. Temperature Records
   • 2023 was the hottest year ever recorded, surpassing the previous record set in 2016.
   • The global mean temperature anomaly was +1.45°C relative to the 1850–1900 baseline.
   • Twelve of the thirteen hottest months on record occurred between June 2023 and May 2024.

2. Greenhouse Gas Concentrations
   • Atmospheric CO₂ reached 421 ppm in 2023, the highest in over 3 million years.
   • Methane (CH₄) levels are approximately 2.6 times higher than pre-industrial concentrations.
   • Nitrous oxide (N₂O) has risen 24% above pre-industrial levels.

3. Ocean Changes
   • Global mean sea level has risen ~20 cm since 1900, with the rate accelerating to 4.5 mm/year.
   • Ocean heat content reached record highs in all measured layers.
   • Ocean acidification has increased by ~26% (0.1 pH unit drop), threatening marine ecosystems.
   • Sea surface temperatures in the North Atlantic broke all previous records in 2023.

4. Ice and Cryosphere
   • Arctic sea ice extent continues to decline at ~13% per decade.
   • The Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets are losing mass at accelerating rates.
   • Mountain glacier retreat is observed on every continent.

5. Extreme Weather Events
   • Heatwaves: frequency has doubled; intensity has increased significantly.
   • Heavy precipitation: rainfall intensity up ~7% per 1°C of warming (Clausius-Clapeyron).
   • Tropical cyclones: evidence for increased proportion reaching Category 4–5.
   • Droughts: compound hot-dry events are more frequent and prolonged.

Projected Scenarios
-------------------
Under the IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (AR6) scenarios:

Low-emission scenario (SSP1-2.6):
  - Warming likely limited to 1.5–2.0°C by 2100.
  - Sea level rise of 0.3–0.6 m by 2100.
  - Requires net-zero CO₂ emissions by ~2050.

High-emission scenario (SSP5-8.5):
  - Warming could reach 3.3–5.7°C by 2100.
  - Sea level rise of 0.6–1.0 m (or higher with ice-sheet instability).
  - Irreversible tipping points highly likely (Amazon dieback, West Antarctic Ice Sheet collapse).

Tipping Points
--------------
Several Earth system tipping elements may already be approaching critical thresholds:
- Greenland Ice Sheet: potential irreversible loss at ~1.5°C sustained warming.
- West Antarctic Ice Sheet: marine ice-sheet instability may be triggered at ~2°C.
- Amazon Rainforest: dieback possible if deforestation exceeds 20–25% (currently ~17%).
- Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC): risk of weakening significantly.
- Arctic Sea Ice: summer ice-free conditions possible before 2040.
- Permafrost thaw: releases additional CH₄ and CO₂, creating a positive feedback loop.

Policy Recommendations
-----------------------
1. Rapid Decarbonisation: achieve net-zero CO₂ emissions globally by mid-century.
2. Methane Reduction: cut methane emissions 30% by 2030 (Global Methane Pledge).
3. Nature-Based Solutions: halt deforestation; restore 30% of degraded land and ocean.
4. Adaptation Investment: increase adaptation finance to at least $300 billion/year by 2030.
5. Just Transition: support vulnerable nations and communities in climate transition.
6. Technology Deployment: accelerate renewable energy, green hydrogen, and carbon capture.

Conclusion
----------
The window to limit warming to 1.5°C is narrowing rapidly. Current nationally determined contributions (NDCs) are insufficient and put the world on a path to ~2.5–2.9°C by 2100. Immediate, deep, and sustained emissions reductions across all sectors are essential to avoid the most catastrophic climate impacts. The science is unequivocal: the actions taken this decade will shape the climate trajectory for centuries.
